NMDA receptors are nonlinear activation functions

November 17, 2022

Original Paper

The hippocampus is a region of the brain essential for consolidation and retrieval of long term memories, as well as spatial/navigational memory required for localization tasks. Studying this part of the brain led to the discovery of place cells, which (kind of) resemble the function of nonlinear activations in neural networks.

Place cells act collectively to form place fields [think feature maps in convolutional neural networks]. Their firing-patterns correlate strongly with environmental stimuli like familiar visual, olfactory, and vestibular cues. In one experiment with rats in a maze, place cells were monitored and each cell gave rise to its own mode in recorded action potentials. The modes were distinguished only by the rat’s position in the maze.

Crucial to the function of these cells (and consequently, the formation of long-term memories) are the self-regulating dynamics of the NMDA receptor and its blockade of Mg2+ ions at resting potential. An influx of cations through the receptor is thought to be a primary mechanism for cellular learning, since Ca2+ and Na+ go on to bond with and influence the behavior of a number of essential proteins. However, this influx is activity dependent:

  1. Unblocking the ion channel requires depolarizing the neuron enough to dislodge the Mg2+ ions, which typically happens thru ligand binding

  2. Once the Mg2+ ions are dislodged, the influx of ions is dependent on the voltage across the cellular membrane.

In the context of neural networks, a negative gradient implies some new event/data point was experienced, and this event does not fit the hypothesis represented by the network at that state. In most cases, a single new sample should not warrant a huge parameter adjustment that overwrites what was previously learned. It is more likely that the sample is an outlier - so with the GELU activation, a network can avoid committing this new information to the hypothesis it represents. Analagously, increased Mg2+ concentration in the brain leads to enhanced long-term memory formation.

The similarity between the graphs is easy to point out, and in this paper the authors propose an NMDAR-like activation function derived from the curve in Figure 1. They evaluate its impact by substituting it for the GELU activation in the transformer architecture, and design a 2D navigation task to assess working and long-term memory. Their results show that place cell representations emerge in the MLP layers of the transformer, the reliance on “long-term memory” can be modulated by changing the nonlinearity of the NMDAR activation. Zero initial Mg2+ concentration corresponds to a linear activation function, and the limit as Mg2+ concentration –> \inf corresponds to a ReLU activation.

It’s interesting also to note how they are operationalizing “working” vs “reference” (long-term) memory in the transformer: self-attention layers corresponding to the former, and FFNs to the latter. It’s again very similar to the analogy of the episodic buffer and long-term memory store in Baddeley’s working memory model.
