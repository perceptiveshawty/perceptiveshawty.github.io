<!-- # Is the depiction of amnesia in *50 First Dates* faithful to any real memory deficit? -->


Adam Sandler and Drew Barrymore are the stars of *50 First Dates*, playing the main characters Henry and Lucy. The plot centers around Henry’s attempts to make a lasting impression on Lucy, who has been suffering from Goldfield’s Syndrome - a fictitious condition that somewhat resembles anterograde amnesia - since a car accident that took place on her Dad’s birthday prior to the movie's events. 

Lucy’s condition causes her memory to ‘reset’ when she sleeps, but allows her to retain information throughout the day prior to sleep. Lucy’s father and brother, throughout the year since her accident, have taken on the burden of supporting Lucy by allowing her to re-enact the same day of her accident, every single day. They were very worried about the implications of maintaining this delusion for so long, and don’t know how she will go on with life many years later - Henry’s commitment to and subtle impact on Lucy’s well-being causes them to warm up and allow him a bigger role in her life. 

The movie climaxes when she receives a ticket over her expired license plate, where she subsequently finds how much time has really passed and ultimately the details of her condition. The rest of the movie chronicles Henry’s creative approaches to helping her cope with her reality, and concludes years into the future where it's implied that Henry’s methods have allowed her to live a happy life and even start a family.

## *Goldfield’s Syndrome*

Although the verbal description of the disorder that Lucy suffers from is identical to that of anterograde amnesia, the essence of/distinction between short and long term memory has/have been blurred in the movie. 

Studies done [1] [2] on the hypothetical short term memory construct have defined short term memory capacity to be between 4-10 basic pieces of information, but this is affected by several factors such as rehearsal/chunking efficacy as well as phonological properties in words. The working memory model [3], which elaborates on the multi-store construct and is fundamentally more explanatory through its distinction between different sensory inputs, does not have a ‘short-term’ memory construct, but nonetheless provides no basis for the kind of forgetting that is depicted through Goldfield’s Syndrome in the movie. 

Two of the most well-known cases of amnesia, KC and HM, have been thoroughly studied and led to conclusions about the kinds of memories that are affected. Both KC and HM had issues with declarative memory - which, according to Ribot’s Law [4], is the kind of memory that is least resistant to disruption. Both patients presented a case of amnesia where new episodic and/or semantic memories could not be formed, but a review of studies on HM’s ability to correctly select semantic information when cued with choices has led to the idea of a further distinction between implicit/inferred memory and explicit memory. Both patients were unable to recall episodes or facts of their volition, but were more successful on tasks where recall was cued by partial information [5] [6]. 

Because several facets of Lucy’s condition are inconsistent with the real-life ailment it most closely resembles, fabricating this disease was probably a good choice for the movie. The information provided by the doctor about the injury (temporal lobe damage) is consistent with structures believed to play a role in anterograde amnesia, and the doctor’s description of 10 Second Tom (who cannot retain memory longer than 10 seconds) suffering from a much worse case of amnesia is more or less realistic. 

If not for developments in the implicit/explicit memory distinction, it would be hard to believe that the happy ending for Lucy could actually happen. However, it is entirely possible that memory degradations are not necessarily the result of disrupted encoding, but a lack of access to the encoded memory i.e. losing the ‘key’ with respect to the index model of the hippocampus. The sudden change in Lucy’s routine, her subtle improvements in her ability to handle her story which she must relearn every day - it is unlikely, but possible for these changes to happen, just as HM was able to slowly form implicit memories relating facts and episodes about his doctor. 

## Remembering Patient FL

Earlier it was suggested that Goldfield’s Syndrome, which bears no perfect resemblance to any known memory disorder, isa warped idea of anterograde amnesia. However, a strange case study [7] has been documented on a patient FL who presented amnesia symptoms exactly how they are depicted by Lucy’s character. Similar to Lucy’s exposition, FL experienced an automobile accident in 2005, sustained an injury that led to impaired short-term memory (the same as Goldfield’s), and was administered several neuropsychological exams to validate concerns about the authenticity of her unique set of symptoms. It was noted that she was a fan of Drew Barrymore, and the events of the movie took place around the same time as her accident, but she claimed she had never seen it. 

FL was able to remember events of the same day when formally tested, but completely failed to recall information from previous days on similar tests of explicit declarative memory. However, they found that when new material was covertly mixed into the learning for the day, she was capable of remembering that information days later - in a sense, her success with covert testing is similar to what was found with HM and the difference in his capacity to recall implicit and explicit memories. To directly address the questionable authenticity of her symptoms, a control group of subjects that were instructed to pretend like they had Goldfield’s Syndrome were administered the same formal tests, and FL’s results differed from theirs in a number of ways. Between these experiments, and otherwise ordinary MRI scans and psychiatric examinations, researchers concluded that FL exhibited a novel form of amnesia that could have been influenced by knowledge of its depiction in the film. 

A single case study certainly does not warrant the claim that Goldfield’s Syndrome is an accurate depiction of amnesia, but nonetheless presents a scientifically valid account of the symptoms depicted in the movie. Between this case study, and several studies on the distinct bases of explicit and implicit memory, *50 First Dates* has its issues with the way it depicts more common forms of amnesia, but doesn’t stray too far from the boundary between science and fiction.
